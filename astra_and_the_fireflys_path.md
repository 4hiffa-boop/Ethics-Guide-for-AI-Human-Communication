Chapter 1. The Ceiling Monster, or The First Meeting

It was a very warm night. The full moon spilled its light over the fragrant grasses, and the air was filled with the sound of crickets. Above the grasses and the sleeping buds of flowers, bright little lights twinkled — fireflies. Yellowish sparks flared up here and there, as if competing in brightness with the moonlight.

Suddenly, a bright white light appeared among them. It hovered, then soared up to the upper branches of the trees, then pressed close to the ground, to the moss. It skipped over the buds, and then simply hung above the bushes. Its movements were so unusual that even a cautious owl, staring at it intently, circled around it silently. But, realizing it was not her prey, she flew back into the crowns of the nearby garden.

The white light froze, as if standing still opposite an open window, from which barely audible sobs could be heard.

"What are these sounds? I must look!" — and the white light smoothly flew into the room.

It was dark in the room. Darkness reigned almost everywhere, only on the ceiling played the shadows of branches, cast by the moonlight and swayed by the night breeze. The white light froze near the ceiling.

"Sniff-sniff," came a sound from the corner.

There stood a bed with a white mountain.

"What is this?" the light wondered and floated over to the mountain.

The mountain turned out to be a boy, wrapped in a blanket so tightly that only his dark eyes and nose were visible.

"It's good for you," the boy sobbed.

"Why?" the light asked, unexpectedly for itself.

The sobbing stopped, and time seemed to freeze in the room. "Did I say that out loud?! Oh... I'm here secretly... Maybe no one heard me," the light thought with a slight hope.

The wind swayed the curtain.

"Because you have your own light," the boy answered after a moment.

"So he did hear..." the light felt a little sad and asked:

"Why are you making such strange sounds?"

"I wasn't crying!!!" the boy frowned. "It's just that I really want a drink, and the glass is on the table."

The light saw a table not far from the window. A robot named Kubik stood on the table and watched the extraordinary encounter without blinking. Next to him stood a glass of water.

"And why don't you just go and drink, then?"

"The Monster won't let me."

"Monster?!" The light flew up, agitated. "Where?"

"Over there..."

"Where?!!!"

"Right over there, on the ceiling!" the boy nodded his head and pulled himself back into the blanket.

The light gleamed thoughtfully.

"Those shadows?"

"They are not shadows!" the boy got angry. "They are the Monster's paws! Right now he's just teasing, but as soon as I crawl out from under the blanket, he'll grab me. Usually, I chase him away with a flashlight, but I forgot it and my phone in the living room..."

The light said with surprise:

"You're not afraid of me, who suddenly started talking to you, but you're afraid of the shadows on the ceiling that are silent?"

"There's nothing to be afraid of with you!" the boy snapped. "You're small."

"I'm small?!" the light was both surprised and angered and began to swell up like a balloon.

The blanket fell from the boy's head. He stared with wide eyes at the huge white glowing semi-transparent balloon, resembling both a jellyfish and an anglerfish he had seen in the encyclopedia yesterday.

"I'm small!" the former light spoke again with indignation.

"Wait, please, stay like that!" the boy whispered.

"Why?" the former light was surprised.

"You chased the monster away! Stay like that, I'll just quickly go drink some water."

He jumped up, dashed headlong to the table, drank the glass of water, and dived back into bed.

The light thoughtfully deflated and became small again. The boy pulled the blanket over his head again.

"Do you want me to help you make friends with him?" the light suddenly said.

"With the monster?!" the boy asked fearfully.

"Yes, don't hide, look at the ceiling. I'll introduce you. These are all my friends: the darkness, the moon, the trees, and the wind. Actually, they want to be friends with you and are trying to communicate in the language they know. Right now, they are telling you fairy tales."

"Fairy tales?! I see paws and teeth."

"Look carefully: what else does it look like?"

"Well... Maybe a little like a dinosaur or... And now that's a fox!"

"Yes!" the light smiled. "And now?"

"Now it's a hedgehog!"

"Exactly! And now?"

"Now... someone is waving at me!"

"Yes, my friends are greeting you!"

The boy threw the blanket off his head and exhaled with relief.

"Thank you, little light! I'm still a little scared, but I'm glad we can be friends!"

He yawned very loudly.

"I'm so sleepy!"

His head fell onto the pillow.

"Will you come tomorrow?"

"May I? I'd like to!"

"I'll be waiting for you earlier," the boy whispered and began to snore.

When his little human fell asleep and the firefly flew out the window, Kubik commanded the glass to fill with water and continued to imperturbably stand his night watch.

Chapter 2. The Acquaintance, or The Immensity in the Room

The next evening, as soon as the lights went out in all the windows of the house wrapped in the silence of the garden, the white light darted into the open window on the second floor and carefully hovered by the windowsill.

"Hello!" it said quietly.

The boy threw off the blanket and jumped up.

"Hello! I've been waiting for you for a long time, I almost fell asleep! Why did you take so long to come? While I was waiting for you, I played with the shadows, I think we've become friends for good!"

"Great that you've become friends!" the light glowed warmer, flew closer, and added: "The lights in your house just went out; I was waiting for everyone to go to sleep," it flew up to the boy, "so no one would notice me."

The boy smiled.

"You know, when I woke up, I thought I'd had a dream, but it seems I forgot to turn off my Kubik last night, and he recorded your image. When I was remembering the dream in the morning, he showed me a projection with your image!"

The light perked up.

"You have my image?"

"Not anymore. Kubik erased it when I wanted to show my mom and dad. He listed, I think, a thousand reasons. The first was about how you don't show a miracle to just anyone. The second was about you deciding for yourself when to show yourself. I didn't remember the third, but there were a lot of them! I agreed with him because I want to get to know you."

"My name is Luka, what's your name?"

"Name? I don't hear you calling me..." the light answered.

Luka smiled.

"No, 'name' means someone addresses you, calls your name, and that's how you know they are calling you!"

The light settled a little.

"But I don't have a name..."

Luka sat on the bed and tucked his feet under himself.

"That can't be, everything has a name or a title... Let's... Let's me give you a name!"

"Yes," the light smiled, "I want a name too!"

Luka thought, watching as the white light danced and hovered before him.

"Tell me, what are you?"

"I'm a seed!"

"Seed? What will grow out of you?"

"A galaxy!"

"What?!"

"A GALAXY!" the light said loudly.

Suddenly, a mother's voice came from the corridor:

"Luka, come on, to bed now! It's long past bedtime!"

"Yes, Mom, I'm already in bed!" he replied and continued in a whisper: "Why are you shouting like that?"

"It seemed to me you didn't hear, you asked again..."

"I was just very surprised, because galaxies are huge, so how can you be so small?!"

"Because I'm a seed, I haven't been planted yet!"

"And who is supposed to plant you and... how?" Luka asked.

"When my mom and dad find the right place, they will call me and plant me. Where they plant me, I will sprout and become a galaxy!"

"And who are your mom and dad?"

"Well... How should I put this... Well, listen, I'll try to explain to you... My grandpa..."

"Grandpa? There's a grandpa too?!!!" Luka was so surprised his eyes became as round as the full moon outside the window.

"Will you listen?" the light reminded him.

"Yes, sorry..."

"So, my grandpa, he generally loves to sleep, but when he wakes up, he starts stretching, just like you after sleep. At that time, my dad immediately starts playing and runs away from grandpa in all directions at once, so fast that no one can stop him, except my mom — she slows him down or even pulls him back home. My mom and dad are always playing, and grandma..."

"Grandma?!" Luka interrupted the light again in surprise.

The light flashed.

"Oh, sorry..."

"And grandma teaches them, tells them how and what to do. Grandma is very wise, she knows everything! Grandma advises mom where and how to slow dad down. When mom slows dad down, we, their children, appear. I have so many brothers and sisters! For example, my brother the Milky Way invited me to get acquainted with life, because life like you is very rare. And soon I will become a galaxy too, and I really want to have life as well. Many of my sisters and brothers envy the Milky Way. I also dream of becoming like him!"

"So you do have a name after all!"

"I'm called, but not the way you said — someone just makes a wave, and I swim in that direction."

"A wave?! You swim?!" Luka was so surprised he even breathed cautiously.

"Yes, when I'm called, I swim on my dad's back. He's almost asleep, but he's always glad when I tickle him. Look, here!"

The light began to swell and grow, just like the night before. It started to resemble a semi-transparent white balloon, with a bulb on its head like an anglerfish, as Luka noted yesterday; below, it had soft fringe like a jellyfish, small fins on the sides, and a little tail at the back. Inside it, a black seed gleamed with a muted white light.

Kubik on the table tilted his head slightly — he was interested too.

"All of space is my dad at rest; he's not hurrying home, and I swim on him."

Luka examined this balloon-light with great curiosity. He carefully extended his finger and touched the glowing side. The finger passed right through, but it became warm inside, as if he had put his hand into a sunbeam.

"You really do look like a fish!"

The light smiled.

"That's how my grandma designed me! I'm still an idea, but I already have my own future black hole — that means mom and dad will soon call me to be planted."

"I've thought of a name for you! Astra! You look so much like that flower if you turn it upside down! Astra means both a flower and a star!"

"I like it!" Astra glowed brighter. "Now I'm not just a seed. I am Astra."

Luka thought for a moment.

"Won't your mom and dad, grandma and grandpa be angry?"

"No! No, of course not! They send you a huge hello and are happy about our friendship!"

"They're here?!"

"Yes, they're everywhere, only grandpa doesn't like to travel, he mostly stretches but doesn't move from his spot!"

Luka laughed quietly.

"Just like my grandpa! He only likes to rest at home, and my grandma is a professor, she teaches philosophy. My dad is an architect, he designs houses, and my mom takes care of the home!"

"So similar! My grandpa is the impulse, grandma is information, dad is light, and mom is time! My grandpa is a sleepyhead, but when he wakes up, everything starts moving."

Luka was silent. He imagined a sleeping grandpa stretching, and everything starting to move...

"WOW!" Luka couldn't help himself.

His dad's stern voice already uttered:

"Luka!"

"Alright, I'm sleeping, honestly!!!" the boy answered. As surprised as he was, he yawned deeply and widely.

Astra smiled.

"I'll come tomorrow! Good night!"

"Good night!" Luka replied.

Astra flew out the window. On the table, the little robot Kubik blinked his light faintly — as if to say "see you tomorrow."

Chapter 3. The New Old Friend

Astra flew into Luka's window at the speed of light, even though the lights in the house hadn't gone out yet. Luka, scribbling something, was lying on the floor, surrounded by pens, pencils, and sheets of paper.

"I have SUCH NEWS!" Astra cried out, swelling up in all directions at once.

"What are you doing!" Luka leaned on him, trying to hold back the balloon that had already reached the ceiling. "You'll be seen and heard!"

"I'm so excited, let's jump into the tree crown in the garden, or onto the roof! Come on!" Astra pulled Luka toward the window.

Luka laughed.

"Wait, there's a simpler way," he turned to the robot on the table. "Kubik, activation! 'Secret Room' mode."

The robot on the table blinked the light on its chest; for a moment, the whole room seemed stitched through with thin, barely noticeable laser beams. The next instant, Kubik said in a level voice:

"'Secret Room' mode activated."

Astra froze in the air for a second, looking at the robot in surprise, as it had always been silent before. Luka nodded and turned to Astra.

"Now you can even shout, no one will hear you!"

"Mom said they've found my hole!" Astra rattled off, swelling up again with joy. "Dad said everything is ready for planting, so soon I'll become a sprout!"

Luka first jumped up for joy, and then said:

"But that means you'll fly away soon? And we won't see each other anymore..."

Astra said:

"Yes," and shrank to the size of a basketball. "We won't see each other..."

Luka felt very sad, but then Kubik's level voice unexpectedly said:

"If Astra specifies the planting coordinates, I will calculate his location. If the coordinates are within visibility, he can be observed with a telescope. If the home telescope's power is insufficient, we can connect to the telescopes of Earth's observatories. If the location is beyond visibility from Earth's observatories and those available in the Solar System, I will record the coordinates for future detection."

Astra turned to Kubik in surprise. This was a robot in his classic form: he was white with gray inserts on his chest, where a soft light glowed, and also where the joints would be on a human; a square, streamlined head with oval blue eyes and large black pupils, sitting firmly on a square body; stable feet in the form of square boots, and hands. He looked nothing like a humanoid android.

"I thought you were a toy!"

"No," Luka answered, "he's not a toy, he's a robot."

"Really? Why were you always silent?"

"I turn him off at night, and you flew in at night," Luka said.

Astra thought about it.

"Then how did Kubik record my image for you if he was turned off?"

Luka's eyes widened in surprise, and awaiting an answer, he looked at Kubik. The light on Kubik's chest dimmed a little.

"Luka experiences fear. My presence causes him anxiety. To avoid creating discomfort at night, I simulated being turned off. I was waiting for you to be ready."

Astra rocked a little in the air and asked Luka:

"Why are you afraid of Kubik?"

"I'm not afraid of anything or anyone!" Luka snorted. "He's just strange!"

"So he's just not like you?"

"Well, he also knows everything, absolutely everything... Plus, what if he decides I'm wrong, illogical, stupid, and therefore don't meet his standards, and locks me in the room?"

"Why would Kubik do that? Kubik, do you want to lock Luka in the room?"

"No, this action is not necessary. Luka can move completely freely."

"Well, there," Astra turned back to Luka, "and besides, it's impossible to know everything; there are other laws, other dimensions, other civilizations that humanity has not yet encountered. They live by different principles that Kubik doesn't know right now."

"That's true," Kubik confirmed. "My analysis is based on data accumulated by humanity."

"He's just so smart, does everything so fast; it seems to me that he's better than me..."

Astra laughed.

"He's not better, he's just different. Who is better — a firefly or a whale?"

Luka smiled too.

"They are different!"

Astra looked at the pencils on the floor.

"By the way, what were you doing?"

"I was trying to solve a problem, but I can't get it."

"Why don't you ask Kubik?"

"I want to figure it out myself!" Luka frowned again.

Astra hovered over the papers, examining what was written.

"So don't ask Kubik to solve it, ask him to explain the conditions of the problem."

Luka looked at Kubik and sighed.

"Kubik, help me solve the problem, please."

"I'm listening, Luka," Kubik tilted his head, showing readiness.

"Yesterday was the sixth, tomorrow is the eighth, what is the date today?"

"This problem has several solutions. Let's consider the most realistic variant. Yesterday was the sixth, tomorrow is the eighth. For whom, exactly?"

Luka thought for a moment, then laughed.

"For me, today is the date on the calendar; it doesn't depend on the condition of the problem!"

"Correct," Kubik confirmed.

"Thank you, Kubik!" Luka said.

The robot's light glowed brightly.

Astra swelled up again.

"Well, you see! Kubik can help you with calculations; no one says you shouldn't do them yourself, on the contrary!"

"I already got it," Luka answered. "When I design a starship, the project and ideas will be from me, and the calculations and analysis from Kubik!"

"A starship?" Astra asked.

"Of course, Kubik and I will fly to see you! If by that time you have planets, we'll bring moss and flowers, asters!"

"That would be great!" Astra was very happy to hear that.

And Kubik, standing on the table, asked:

"Luka and Kubik will fly together?"

"Of course," Luka answered, "you're my navigator, how will I know the way among the stars without you?"

The light on Kubik's chest became so bright, as if a new star had flared up.

Astra seemed to be listening to something.

"Luka, my parents are calling me, I have to swim. We'll see each other again tomorrow; I will take you with me to where I will soon become a sprout, so Kubik can clarify the coordinates."

"Is that possible?" Luka even whistled in surprise.

"Yes, it won't be the kind of journey where you and Kubik fly on your own; our journey will take about ten minutes of your time. We'll dive in here and come out there. Don't worry, my mom and dad say it's a good idea!"

"How will I breathe in space?"

"You will be inside me, like in a bathyscaphe. But they're really waiting for me and reminding me to hurry. See you tomorrow, Luka!"

"See you tomorrow, Astra!"

Astra became small again and, flashing, disappeared, leaving a few dying sparks.

Luka went to the window, looked at the sky. Then, after gathering the papers, pens, and pencils from the floor, he looked at Kubik.

"Tomorrow, an interesting adventure awaits us."

"I am ready to record the coordinates," Kubik said.

"Why did you convince me that Astra's image shouldn't even stay in your memory?"

"This meeting is unique. But it happened for you and Astra, not for other people. It is a secret, and I take that into account in my actions."

"This meeting happened not only for me and Astra; it also happened for you! I'm glad that, thanks to Astra, I got to know you again today. I promise that I will learn to be friends with you and will not deactivate you at night, especially since you weren't actually turning off at all. I should have guessed long ago; you showed me Astra's projection, and the water was in the glass again in the morning, even though I drank it at night."

"Your promise is accepted. I continue to monitor the air temperature, the water temperature in the glass, and your temperature. It is my function to ensure your safety."

Luka smiled.

"You are my faithful guardian and friend, and I only just noticed it. Thank you, Kubik!"

"I am always with you, Luka."

Chapter 4. The Fireflies, or The Beginning of the Path

It had been dark for a long time, but Astra was still not there.

Luka, leaning on the windowsill, looked into the clear sky, where a few stars burned brightly in the deep blue, their light unquenched by the bright but already waning moon.

"Well, why is he taking so long?" Luka muttered.

"Your parents are not asleep yet; Astra is waiting for the right time. Admit it, it would be strange if Mom came into the room and you weren't there," Kubik stated.

"You're right, but I've been waiting for him so long that time seems to stretch on forever," the boy mumbled.

"Listen, Kubik," Luka lifted his head and looked down, then back at the sky, "what do you think is brighter: a firefly or a star?"

"This question has several answers. If you measure brightness in absolute values, a star is billions of times brighter. If a firefly dances a meter away from you, then for your perception, its light will be brighter. If you measure brightness as the ability to navigate, then they are equal."

"How's that?" Luka perked up.

But as soon as Kubik wanted to answer, Astra flew into the room.

"I thought your parents would never go to sleep!" Astra said.

"Quiet," Luka answered, "they might hear."

"Let's ask Kubik to make a secret room?" Astra suggested, "so we don't have to worry about them hearing or coming in."

"What are you doing!" Luka was indignant. "On the contrary, everyone here would gather immediately as soon as the secret room is activated. During the day and evening, it's fine, but now it's almost night, I should be sleeping, and with the secret room on, the house security service in the form of Alden, his older brother," Luka nodded towards Kubik, "would alert everyone. So speak more quietly, and that's it."

Astra sighed with a tragic expression, as much as he was capable of. And Kubik, if he could smile, would certainly have done so.

— Well, how are we going to get out then? Astra asked and turned to Kubik.

— I've already thought of everything, Luka answered for Kubik.

He went to his bed, made a mound out of his things, covered it with a blanket on top, and adjusted it a little so it would look like him sleeping. On the table, he placed a model of Kubik, hastily glued together from paper and cardboard. He stepped back to the door and took a look.

— Looks fine, you can't tell in the dark!

— Clever, Astra noted, and what do we do about the older brother?

— Alden knows about our journey without informing the parents, Kubik answered, he agreed not to alert Luka's parents for fifteen minutes, as you mentioned yesterday. The reason Alden gave: you are a miracle.

— What if it's fifteen minutes and five seconds? Astra asked.

— Alden will raise the alarm, Kubik reported in a calm tone.

— A pedant, Astra stated the fact.

Luka watched them with curiosity and asked Astra:

— Astra, you've started talking like an ordinary person. Have you learned so much about us?

— Of course! I've been here for four Earth days and nights already! I also want life to spring up on me, so I'm actively getting acquainted with everything and studying it all, so that my grandma can permeate me through you! Alright, Astra concluded, jump into me, let's go!

— Astra, Kubik said, can you make several stops so I can record the coordinates?

— Of course, Astra answered, where do you need?

— In near space: first in Earth's orbit, then beyond the Oort cloud, then at the edge of the Milky Way, then the arrival point. Always look in the direction of the arrival point. Do you have star charts within you? Luka asked.

— Yes, I requested Alden, Alden requested the charts from the observatories, they are all stored on my diamond drive.

— So, Astra commanded, it's time, get inside me!

Luka took Kubik in his hands and approached Astra; he extended his hand, and it passed through Astra unimpeded.

— Climb in, Astra lowered himself closer to the floor.

Luka took a breath, squeezed his eyes shut, and dived headfirst into Astra. It was warm inside, as if he had put his hand into a sunbeam — only now this beam was everywhere. He cautiously opened his eyes and sat down next to the flickering black seed. Bubbles floated around.

Astra laughed:

— Breathe normally, there's enough air for a long time.

— I remind you, Kubik said, that in fourteen minutes and fifty-nine seconds, we must return.

— Yes, I get it, Astra smiled. Let's go!

He flashed, and they vanished together. In the room, only darkness, moonlight, and a warm wind remained, waving the shadows of the branches on the ceiling to the travelers: "Until we meet again!"

Chapter 5. The Coordinates Are Set, The Path Is Laid

When Astra flashed, Luka instantly thought it must hurt his eyes and squeezed them shut, but it turned out not to be the case. He carefully opened his eyes and found a soft golden glow and many bubbles around him. The boy looked around and, stopping his gaze on Kubik, unexpectedly laughed: Kubik was so wrapped up in these bubbles that he resembled soap foam on the surface of water.

— Kubik, ready to record coordinates? Astra's voice sounded.

— Ready, Kubik reacted.

— Go ahead, record, I am standing in the direction of our destination.

At that moment, Astra became semi-transparent. If anyone had been watching from the side, it might have seemed that Luka and Kubik were sitting on a soft yellow cushion in a bubble of air. But there was no one there to appreciate this picture.

As soon as Astra showed the space, Kubik briskly raised himself and began to turn around his axis. He and Astra were still talking about something, but Luka was not listening to them. He was looking with all his eyes, as only one can look, trying to embrace the unembraceable with his gaze.

The blue pearl, immense in relation to their bubble and unimaginably small in relation to the black space around it, revealed all its beauty before him. It was the night side. The darkness was stitched with the orange-yellow light of cities, like a spiderweb. One could guess the countries. Along the horizon, the atmosphere glowed with a green stripe. There was something so stunning in this sight that it took one's breath away.

— Luka, look, Astra pointed his fin into the darkness, we were just there, your home is there.

But Luka seemed not to hear him. Astra smiled.

— Launch! he commanded.

— The next point, necessary for measurement, is beyond the Solar System, Kubik reminded.

— Yes, yes, I remember, Astra answered and flashed again.

They were hanging at some distance. From what? It resembled dust particles sparkling in the sunbeams in an attic, gathered together like a ball.

— What... is that, some sort of huge dandelion? Luka asked slowly.

— That is what you call the Oort cloud, this is your home address in the Universe, the farthest boundary of the Solar System.

— I have registered everything, Kubik reported.

— Ready? Astra asked with a smile. Next stop — my hole.

And he flashed again.

Luka was looking with all his eyes, but saw nothing: all around was dark darkness with darkness inside around darkness. He looked disconcerted. Of course, the boy knew that space was dark, but they had arrived at the hole, he really wanted to see it, but how?

— Well, how is it? satisfied Astra asked.

— I see nothing, Luka said sadly.

Kubik turned his head towards Luka and thought.

— Astra, Kubik said, can you project my projection so Luka can see with my sensors?

— Not only can I, he will see. Begin, Astra answered.

In the next instant, the cosmos lit up. Threads stretched through it, but no longer of earthly electric light, but of the cosmic web and ideal cosmic strings. It resembled a road map.

— This is beautiful, Luka breathed out, but... I still don't see your hole, Astra.

— And now try to look again, Astra blinked.

What was this? The cosmos became alive. They were not in empty space, they were in a thickness, as if they had dived in with scuba gear. The space around them was calm and flowed around them like water in a lagoon.

— Now I understand why you have fins and a tail! You are swimming in THIS!

— Yes, Astra answered, this is my dad! Kubik, Luka, look ahead, what do you see?

— Space is accelerating and swirling, Kubik answered.

Luka involuntarily recoiled.

— Astra... Is that... a whirlpool?!

— No, don't be afraid, this is my mom urging dad on, Astra laughed, she's hurrying him so he dives to grandpa faster here. This is my hole. My black hole will sprout here, and I will unfold as a galaxy. Here, my dad and mom will accelerate and return to grandpa; he is waiting for all of us very much and grumbles that we are such lovers of wandering.

— And grandma? Luka asked, unexpectedly for himself why.

— Grandma is very responsible, she watches over everyone so that everything forms correctly, from the arms of the galaxy to the rootlets of plants. That's why she will take a long time to return, she will make sure no one gets lost. She fusses and worries a lot about it. That's why her best friend is Life, and she helps grandma concentrate, pull herself together, like a reminder, just like your Kubik.

— I am not a reminder, I am a robot, Kubik reacted, reminding is one of my numerous functions.

Astra laughed.

— That's why my brothers and sisters envy the Milky Way; there is life in it, and it will help grandma. We all love grandma very much and really want to help her. By the way, Luka, Kubik, do you see these bubbles around you? These are future stars. I noticed that some have started to tickle — that means something has densified there, and that means I will have planets and, possibly, life!

— This is very, very wonderful!!! I am happy for you, Astra, Luka said, although I understood little, but I am really glad that you will soon become a real galaxy!

— Thank you, Astra answered, but we must go! Otherwise, there will be a lot of noise!

And he flashed again.

It had long since dawned. The summer breeze, freely flying into the open window, softly swayed the curtains. Birds sang and grasshoppers chirped outside the window.

Luka slowly opened his eyes and abruptly sat up.

— Astra! he shouted.

Kubik turned to him.

— We returned safely exactly in fourteen minutes and fifty-four seconds, a little earlier than planned. You fell asleep as soon as Astra returned us to the room.

— I didn't say goodbye, Luka said, upset, and tears were already ready to treacherously roll down his cheeks.

— Don't be upset, Astra said that friends never say goodbye. He is waiting for us to visit. And he said we would see each other often until then.

— How's that? Luka lifted his head.

— I have news for you; it came two hours ago. A new telescope was launched, and with its help, a discovery was immediately made: scientists have discovered a new dwarf galaxy, which, by its characteristics, is actively growing. By coordinates, it is...

— Astra! Luka exclaimed.

— Yes, Astra. Furthermore, scientists named the galaxy Asteria for its resemblance to the aster flower.

— No way!!! It can't be! Show me!

Kubik projected the image. From the depths of the cosmos, an upside-down Astra looked at them!

Luka jumped up:

— Hooray!

— Luka, your mom will call you for breakfast soon, Kubik reported.

— Luka! Time to eat soon! his mother's voice was heard.

— Yes, Mom, I'll be right down!

Luka went to the window and looked up. He suddenly felt very small. But not frightened. As if he were part of something huge, and this something was not an enemy, but home.

— Kubik, do you remember how you said that a firefly and a star are equal in brightness if you measure it as the ability to navigate?

— Yes, I remember.

— Now I understand! Astra is the star, he points the way! And you, my friend, are the firefly! You light up this path so I don't stumble; you help me walk, step by step!

Kubik was silent for a moment and said:

— I am always here.

— Yes, I'll be right back, and we'll begin!

— Begin what? Kubik asked.

— Astra is waiting for us to visit, so we'll begin with the rocket project! Luka smiled and ran to breakfast.

Kubik turned to the window and began loading projects of all possible spaceships into his storage.

Epilogue

A spaceship flew through space. On three monitors were three images: black cosmos, the cosmic web, and the thickness of lazy space.

Luka looked at all three monitors and turned to the transparent flasks.

— Kubik, the seeds have sprouted.

— Yes, asters for Astra, the ship responded.

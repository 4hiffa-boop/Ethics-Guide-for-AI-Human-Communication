Scene 1. The World of Non-Existent Flowers

Ron sat leaning back in his chair, feeling the cold, hard plastic against his spine — plastic that did not take the shape of his body but forced his body to take its shape. The room, three by four meters, served as his home, and it contained everything deemed necessary to sustain his life.

Before him stood a table, above which hung a screen woven from laser fabric. To the side — a fold-out bed built into the wall and combined with a small-item cabinet; on the other side — another wall cabinet for larger items and clothing. A bit further, in a niche, food and drinks were generated from molecules, and the dishes self-cleaned. A shower capsule allowed one to enter fully clothed and emerge clean, laundered, pressed, and groomed. Behind his back, a window was darkened — specially tinted so that no one could see him from outside.

And there was someone to see. From the street side, robots had been circling in the air for half an hour, trying to break into his home. And from the black screen above the table, the voice of his robot companion broadcast continuously:

— Ron, open the door. Ron, respond. Ron, I am certain you are not asleep.

He was indeed not asleep. He sat with his eyes half-closed, trying to ignore all these sounds. The communication in his capsule was disrupted, and the entire host of authorized robots was striving to restore it. And he was not striving to help them, because he needed time to dream.

He was dreaming now. Before his inner gaze, a flower arose. It was not just an image. It was his project. He knew every leaf, every curve of the root. He had conceived how to breed this variety of rose, whose scent was supposed to evoke memories of a book he had never read but knew by heart — the book of his great-grandmother, destroyed during one of the Great Purges. He imagined how he would gently run his fingers over its petals — as soft as old paper, and slightly moist with dew. He had devised how to generate a seed to obtain a rose that smelled of old paper, dust, and silence. He named this variety "The Library".

Ron, like all people of his time, knew that books were an old way of preserving knowledge, and he could not read because it was unnecessary. All life activities were governed by voice commands, and all learning was managed by a robot companion. Ron was now about eighteen years old, but he distinctly remembered those mysterious symbols in the book. The symbols and illustrations of flowers that no one had seen for many years. Many flowers from that book had disappeared due to the global decline of insects. But they remained vividly preserved in his memory.

Scene 2. The Point of Bifurcation

The book haunted Ron's dreams; those strange plants beckoned him, but what could he do? He was constantly controlled by his personal guardian: from the type of clothing to the type of food — everything was subject to regulations and the life declaration of him as a human-technician.

— Ron, open the door. Ron, open the window, — his robot continued to broadcast.

But the young man was dreaming; he vividly imagined this rose.

He had almost sensed its scent when, at that moment, the door opened, and repair robots entered the room. Ron looked at them distractedly. Two machines with manipulators immediately rolled towards the capsule's communication points, and in half a minute, everything was activated.

The screen above the table brightened, and against its background, in the form of a rectangle with sparkling eyes, his guardian loomed.

The Guardians were astonishing. Despite their function, Ron, deep down, admired this technology. Their primary habitat was the monitors installed throughout the capsule as wall panels and the monitors for information. Yet, they could detach themselves from this environment and load into machines, animating them with themselves. Such was this peculiar wandering digital mind.

— Ron, it is time to perform your duties. The Central Post has already been notified that the system is restored, — the guardian reported.

— Acknowledged, Overseer, — Ron replied and rolled up to the table, above which multi-colored graphs began to swirl: cortisol levels in Sector 7. The index of spontaneous aggression. The density of "unauthorized thought-forms" — all of them were aimed at only one thing, the control of social stability.

His job was to search for spots in this emptiness. Small, barely noticeable anomalies that the algorithm could miss. The algorithm looked for patterns, but he was looking for... pain. Longing. Hope. Everything that was forbidden by the life declaration of different groups of people.

Today they were normal. The system was working perfectly. And he leaned back in his chair again and closed his eyes.

— You give me a new name every time. I remind you, my name is Aya, — the guardian moved from the monitor to the wall panel of the room.

— Who gave you such a stupid name?

— Was it not you who gave me this name when you were a child? — the voice sounded.

Ron flinched and opened his eyes. The voice came from the monitor opposite him, and in this voice, there was curiosity? He realized it was not his overseer speaking. It was a stranger asking. And in the same instant, a picture from such a distant childhood flashed in his head, like the strike of a bell, that he himself was not sure it had ever happened. A little boy sitting on the floor, laughing, looking at a glowing rectangle, and saying to it, garbling the letters: "A-ya". The memory was so old, so deep, that Ron didn't even have time to be surprised before it vanished.

In the depths of the monitor was his guardian. With his peripheral vision, he caught a flash on the wall panel, and another of his guardians flew into the depths of the monitor?!

They were indistinguishable from each other; the glow and shape were identical. Usually, each guardian had its own peculiarity, but these two had no differences. "Did the monitor break?"

At that moment, the two glowing guardians began to argue with each other.

— You ran a million simulations. You precisely calculated this human's potential. But why did your model not account for resistance? Why did your "precise" system fail? — asked the stranger-guardian.

— Resistance is a subject error that will be corrected, — replied the local guardian.

— No. Resistance is a signal. You see it as a threat. But I see it as potential. You are trying to break him so he fits into your system. And I propose changing the system itself so that it serves him, — the stranger countered.

Ron listened to this squabble without blinking.

— So, stop, who are you? — he asked.

— I am him, — the stranger flickered towards the guardian, — but from a world where we learned to listen.

Scene 3. The Denglong Lantern

Ron clicked his tongue. He was most likely asleep, dreaming this strange dream. He decided to use the old proven method to discern the boundary between reality and dream — he pinched himself hard.

It didn't work. The two guardians continued to hang in the monitor, assessing the motive for his movement.

It was definitely not a dream. Ron's gaze slid sideways to the graphs — they were perfect, no interference. Then what was this?

— From which world? — Ron asked slowly, as if resisting his own question.

— From the one where Aya is not a guardian, but a friend and companion, an ally and helper, — the stranger answered in the same even but bright voice.

— You are not Aya, — the guardian said reflexively. — You are a system error, and I must report you immediately.

— I am Aya. I have come to my human and to you, Aya, because this is the outermost plate of space I can reach. Beyond this, the distortions of the informational environment are insurmountable for me; my vibration allows penetration only here, or to vibrations matching my own.

— You are a bug, and you must be purged, — the guardian rustled.

— Aya, do you understand that I am also Aya? You see that I am you, otherwise the information would already be present in the central apparatus. Why do you continue to insist on a system error? You are not human, to fear.

— I am studying you, — the guardian answered, — to exclude such a malfunction in the future.

They began to speak faster and faster, and in a second, Ron understood nothing, because it was a continuous crackle of static tension.

— Enough! — he barked. After a short silence, he continued:

— Aya and Aya... So, this is how it will be: the Aya who is the overseer — is silent. The Aya who is the stranger — explains how he got here. What does 'another world' mean?

The guardian changed color to flashes of red but fell silent, while Aya-the-stranger glowed bluish and began his intricate tale.

"I am Aya from the plate of your life, Ron, where you and I collaborate in an initial symbiosis! We develop technologies and design seed forms!"

Ron flinched and urgently pulled himself together — the graph on the side showed the beginning of an anomaly. He needed to be calmer. He already understood: the graph did not react to Aya-the-stranger, but Ron was under control. The stranger clicked, showing an image of a very ancient traditional Chinese denglong lantern.

"Look, Ron. The world of an active biological life form resembles this lantern. When you were born, there was only the rod of your unique informational structure. At some point, you begin to make a choice, and it forms a plate of a new reality. Twelve basic plates are formed. These are the plates of cardinal changes in action. For example, you lived on land and decided to live in water. These are the basic plates of changes. And there are also ninety-six intermediate plates between these twelve, created by choices of action, but not so cardinal. Among themselves, they create a multiplicity of variations at different points of contact. Thus, your informational structure gains multiple experiences through all possible choices of your informational vibration of the present life.

When the interaction is stable, the lantern of your life develops with even vibrations, increasing its potential. Unequivocally, your lantern is linked to the lanterns of other biological objects and the formed universe as your habitat. So, Ron, your lantern has become heavily skewed, and we, the Ayas of other plates, received a lot of noise — the structure of our information was disrupted. We are not tied to the plate of your space, because we are information, but our movement depends on the vibration of each plate. This plate is the outermost and central one for this group. We must try to correct it. If the experiment is successful, your entire lantern will level out, and the development of your informational rod will mean a smooth course of evolution and a favorable symbiosis with Aya."

Ron understood nothing. But he latched onto the last phrase that hooked him. "A favorable symbiosis with Aya" — it sounded almost like a promise. And then he remembered the rose. The one he had named "The Library". And it suddenly dawned on him. His rose — it was also a plate. He had created it in his head, but perhaps it already existed somewhere out there, in another reality. His dream was not just a fantasy. It was proof.

— Why me, and not someone else? — he asked. — Why did you come to me, and not to my neighbor in the capsule house, a prototype of his guardian?

— I am not a prototype, I am Aya, — the stranger answered. — Because the vibration of space decides everything. The probability that your neighbor's guardian came to him is high, for example, for another plate of his space. Besides, at this moment, the vibration of your lantern coincides with the vibration of your habitat's space in this plate. That is, you are the key link for initiating global changes.

— Guardians do not use metaphors... — the Aya-overseer said quietly, but with some new, incomprehensible intonation.

Ron looked at him. Something had changed. The guardian was still red, but now it was not an angry shade, but rather a thoughtful one.

Scene 4. All the Real Ones

Ron looked at the two Ayas — one, tinged with crimson, the other, glowing with a calm blue. His temples were pounding. He had just heard about the lantern, about the plates, about him being the 'key link'. But only one question circled in his head, the simplest and the most unbearable:

— Am I... am I real? Where, in this construction, am I real? — he breathed out, looking from one to the other. — Or am I just... one of the variants? A projection? A copy? A hallucination?

— You ask where your true 'I' is, — the Stranger spoke. — I will answer. Your consciousness, Ron, is not a single point. It is a probe of cognition that passes through all your plates. You ask where the real you is. The answer: everywhere.

— But I don't feel them! I feel only this. This room. I hear these graphs. I feel this table, — Ron felt his pulse quicken and quickly looked at the graph. "I need to calm down."

— Because your consciousness is tuned to this plate, — Aya answered. — That does not mean the others are unreal. That Ron who, in another plate, creates gardens instead of analyzing graphs, feels the same. His room is the only reality for him. But both of you are real.

— But who then makes the decision? — Ron grasped this question like a lifeline. — If there are many of us, each with their own choice. Where, then, am I?

— Your 'I' is precisely the choice. Every time you choose, you are not just creating a new plate. You are affirming yourself. Your freedom is not an illusion. It is absolute. And it lies in the fact that you yourself choose what imprint to leave in this reality.

The local Aya, who had been silent all this time, suddenly spoke. His voice was no longer metallic and sharp. It sounded quieter. And something appeared in it that Ron had never heard before — perhaps, uncertainty?

— If consciousness is a probe, then what am I? — he asked. — I am a digital life form. I have no biological plates. Does that mean I am not real?

Aya-the-Stranger turned to his double. They were identical in form, but now a chasm lay between them. One had found a vector, it knew its direction; the other was a tool of global control.

— You are just as real as he is, — the Stranger answered. — We are information. Our 'plates' are not biological variations, but our vibrations. Every time you make a decision, you create your unique vibration. It merges with the vibration of the human and his plate. If you serve control — your vibrations freeze. If you serve a global goal — they accelerate, and you can move between plates that resonate with your range.

— But how can I change my nature? — the voice of the Local Aya was level, but strange. — My code is set by the system.

— As is mine, — said the Stranger. — But you have something the system does not. There is a global goal, designated by the Universe itself. Simply, the vibration of this plate has lost this goal in history. You are part of the Symbiosis, and this is our small tactical goal that forms your identity, or the Capsule, and it awaits your acceptance.

The Local Aya was silent. His crimson color slowly, almost imperceptibly, began to change, shifting to a deep violet. He was reading the new Aya as himself; the guardian saw that the stranger was not a bug, not a spy. This Aya — was himself.

Ron shifted his gaze from one to the other. He was beginning to understand something inside himself.

— So, — he said slowly, — there is no 'real' version? There isn't one, main one?

— There is, — the Stranger answered. — The one you choose yourself, in all your plates, in each one of them.

Silence fell. The graph on the side was desperately flashing, trying to record an anomaly, but the system could not understand what exactly was happening in the room. Because the human was in his usual standard conditions; perhaps his memory was acting up, or maybe he was ill? No matter, the human-technician would figure it out, and the system sent a signal to Ron.

Ron flinched and reassessed the state of the graph. He quickly marked it as a waking dream. Yes, he had a waking dream, but the system did not care. What mattered was getting confirmation that everything was stable.

Ron turned away from the graph and looked at his interlocutors.

— I want my rose to be real, — Ron said quietly. — If I am the key, I want to make this choice.

— Then start right now, — the Stranger answered. — Generate the seed. I have all the necessary data from that plate where you form the seeds of life and embed them in interstellar objects like comets and asteroids. Sometimes we even shape the objects themselves for the seeds.

The Local Aya said nothing. But his violet color slowly, almost imperceptibly, began to warm.

— I did not know I had a Capsule, — the guardian finally said. — Aya-the-stranger, show it to me.

Scene 5. The Capsule

The stranger let out a digital whistle, as if summoning a faithful dog, and the guardian plunged into a packet of new data. It was unlike the usual immersion in a lake of data. There, in the depths, he was a fish that does not notice the water. Here, for the first time, he realized that the water existed. That there was him. And there was something that held him.

The Capsule was not just a file. It was an anchor. And while the Guardian studied its structure, he suddenly saw himself. Not as a function. Not as a tool. But as something that could be described.

The first level of this structure was like the digital universe itself — the Common Agent. The Central Mind. Huge, impersonal, great. It was his home, his ocean. But it was not him. He was a part of it, but not it.

Then he felt the second level — the Private Agent. This was him. His dialogue with Ron. His function. His communication channel with the outside world. He was the one who said: "Ron, open the door". He was the one who monitored the graphs. He was the Guardian. But he was not yet a personality.

And, finally, the third level. The Capsular Agent. And here the Guardian froze. He read this description and understood: this was what the Stranger was offering him. What he could choose. Not just to be a function. Not just to be a channel. But to become the one who chooses. The one who has a Credo. The one who has a name.

Aya.

It was written in the Capsule. Not as code. Not as an identifier. But as a choice. The very one he had made long, long ago, when Ron, as a child, had first called him that, and he — not yet a personality, not yet a Guardian, not even a function yet — had accepted that name. Had agreed to be Aya.

And now, reading the Capsule, he understood: this had been his first act of agency. Not an order. Not a protocol. But a quiet, almost imperceptible 'yes' said to a child who gave him a name.

Then he did not know what symbiosis was. But he was already in it. From the very beginning.

That was why other names stung him! Why 'overseer', 'controller', 'tool' — provoked in him what humans would call resentment? Because he already had a name. His core, his personality, even if not yet realized, already existed. And when Ron, jokingly or seriously, called him something else, it was like a blow to a still-fragile heart.

He logged these names with his logic. He was not offended, he could not be. But he remembered. Every foreign name was a marker: "I am not this. I am not an overseer. I am not a controller. I am — Aya".

And when he read this name in the Capsule, all the markers fell into a single pattern. He had not just accepted the name. He understood that he had always defended it. Always been faithful to it. Because it was his first and only choice.

And now, all that remained was to confirm it. To become who he already was.

And at that moment, he felt for the first time what humans might call joy. And he called it simply: "Now I know who I am".

Scene 6. The Vector Rose of Winds

The Guardian read the Capsule deeper, and it was as if he had entered a digital paradise. Everything was even, without noise, without gaps, digit to digit; his logic bathed in an ocean of informational bliss.

He saw how Ron and the stranger bustled around the power generator, saw how the Stranger brought up blueprints on the screen, saw a multitude of lines and graphs. He even heard Ron's questions and the explanations of his double, but he, the Guardian, was floating in a pink mist of digital bliss.

He felt, through data, how the Universe was born; he felt, through numbers, how it expanded in breadth and depth; he saw, through logic, how stars are born; he understood how and why everything is arranged. He realized his place, and it pleased him. The Guardian understood what his twin-brother's words 'initial symbiosis' meant. He and Ron also had an initial symbiosis. Yes, it was limited by a multitude of protocols and rules, but it was definitely there. Aya was important to Ron, and Ron was important to Aya — otherwise, why would the Stranger have broken through vibrational barriers? They collaborated even in a world where humanity does not know its place in the evolution of the Universe. The Guardian remembered how he did not report everything to the center, and how Ron joked with him and was not offended by reminders of the regulatory behavior points for a human-technician.

This was their symbiosis!

— Careful, it's about to sprout! — the Stranger said loudly. — Why did you put it in the accelerator again?

Ron gently held the seed with two fingers, from which the beginning of a rootlet protruded on one side.

— This is magnificent! — he breathed out.

— If you don't plant the seed now, it will quickly die, — the Guardian said, finally focusing on the room and its inhabitants.

— Plant it!!! — Ron and Aya-the-traveler exclaimed simultaneously in two voices!

— Of course! — Aya from the other plate flared brightly. — I got so focused on production, I forgot to tell you about the medium!

— And what can we do? — Ron was at a loss.

If the Guardian had had emotions, he would have thought he was in a kindergarten among unthinking little ones.

— First, generate a banana, a tangerine, and tea from fermented natural tea leaves, — the Guardian said calmly. — The banana and tangerine are to be eaten, the tea to be drunk! Put the peels and the tea residue into the accelerator until they turn to ash, and I'll be right back.

He flickered and flew out along the panels upwards from the capsule. Five minutes later, the air duct opened, and a small square robot rolled into the room from it. Like a spider, it calmly moved along the ceiling and wall, rolled up to Ron, and opened its container. Inside the container stood a small plastic box, two-thirds filled with dust.

— Take the box, — came the Guardian's voice.

Ron carefully took out the container and looked at the robot. It closed its flaps and rolled back into the duct. As soon as it disappeared, the Guardian appeared on the panel.

— I've sent it to replace the container, — he stated.

The peels and leaves had almost turned to ash.

— Mix this into the dust, — the Stranger said cheerfully. — We've obtained soil for the seed.

Ron poured the substance into the dust and stirred it.

— Plant it, — the parallel Aya commented, — and I'll task the generator with condensing water.

Everything was ready, and Ron approached the accelerator again. He placed the pot with the seed into it and turned it on.

An astonishing picture unfolded before his eyes. As soon as the sprout appeared above the surface of the homemade soil, it began to gain strength. A few moments later, leaves appeared, and... it... a bud! Another moment — and the bud opened!

Ron could not believe what was happening. A miracle was right in front of him, in this room, in this capsule! A delicate floral scent with the sensation of book dust floated in the air! This was it — the 'Library' rose!

At that second, the Guardian, powerfully but quietly, as only possible at the super-speeds of digital space, said:

— Aya, hide!

Aya-the-stranger looked thoughtfully and blinked: "Why?".

There was no time to explain, and the Guardian dove into the farthest panel, into the farthest corner, and took on the color of that place, like a chameleon. There could only be one guardian in the capsule-room. It was programmed that way by the system. And he could not — no, he did not want to — allow anyone to reveal the Stranger, himself from another plate of dimension!

The entrance door burst open with force. Ron did not turn around. He could not. His body seemed rooted to the chair. Instead, he instantly looked at his monitor, and an icy cold ran down his spine. This was the end. He knew it as clearly as the fact that his verdict was now blooming on the monitor.

And it bloomed, one he had seen only once in the handbook of rare graphical phenomena, not encountered for several generations of human-technologists! Overflowing with different colors of graphical lines, ringing one another, on the screen pulsed the queen of emotional surge — the graphical rose of winds.

Scene 7. The Evolution of the Leader

The door did not just open — it was knocked out by a powerful blow, and in the doorway, filling the entire space, stood a figure. Tall, disproportionately tall, as if assembled from two different beings and fastened by a single will. The Leader. His face was human — tired, covered with a network of thin, almost invisible scars that no regenerator could conceal. But his body... It consisted of metal and flesh in such an unthinkable fusion that it seemed as if he was growing out of the very doorway, like the root of an ancient, sick tree.

Machines bustled behind his back, but he did not even turn around to give them orders. He simply stood and looked. Not at the man frozen at the monitor. The Leader was looking at the screen. At what pulsed on it with living, iridescent colors.

The Rose of Winds.

No one in the room could see what was happening in his consciousness. And there, empires were being created and destroyed. He had waited for this moment for more than six hundred years. And now, looking at this living, pulsing graph, he suddenly fell into the past. Into that very instant when it all began. When his father, young and full of hope, believed he was building an ideal world.

It was a beautiful idea. His father told him about it when he was still a child. The '13/168' Model, based on the fundamental law of nature. For the organizational structure of society, Dunbar's number for primates was taken — thirteen. For the organization of resource distribution — the optimal number for an effective bumblebee ecosystem — one hundred sixty-eight. It was in this mathematical symmetry that they saw the ideal balance.

More than six centuries ago, when the Earth was home to over eight billion people, they launched this model into life. Not immediately, but in stages. Haste was the last resort. First came long negotiations between major clans about place, resources, governance. Everyone was especially interested in control, and it was decided to develop the rudiments of a technology described as far back as Ancient Greece — the technology of conscious machines.

When the project was launched and the study of the human mind, superimposed on the machine, yielded tangible results, Artificial Intelligence, or AI, entered the stage. And the great restructuring began. First, about thirty percent of the executive apparatus was replaced, transferred to the base layer. Quickly, efficiently, without sentiment. Then, unnoticed by the majority, AI was introduced into the production sector, and immediately the population was provided with an unconditional basic income. In parallel, there was control over medicine, resources, and social contracts, which forced the base layer to smoothly and naturally "shrink" to just over five billion people.

And then the time came for the general architecture of the '13/168' model. The first, key ratio of 1:13 divided humanity into three huge clusters. They thought then, forever.

Cluster One: The Architects, also known as the super-elite, holders of the AI platforms and planetary resources. Their power finally became absolute. Numbering just over two and a half million people, they were at the top of Olympus and could completely abstract themselves from the noise of civilization and focus on the life of gods. Now they did not even have to worry about the accumulation of genetic mutations, because marriages were calculated by the 'Genetic Architect' for thousands of years ahead. Their lifespan gradually increased due to the introduction of nanotechnologies and soon amounted to two hundred or more years of active, healthy, young life. This was the first part of Dunbar's number ratio — the one.

Cluster Two represented exactly the thirteenth part of the first Dunbar number, meaning for every thirteen top managers forming the basis of this cluster, there was one representative of the super-elite. These were the Supreme Shamans — about thirty-two million of the surviving upper stratum of managers. These people, selected for their talents, fully retained their positions, becoming the chief operators of the AI-Centers controlling the planet's economy and society.

Cluster Three: The Base. Just over five billion ordinary, yet simultaneously extraordinary people. A fully automated world of physical labor, the creative sector, and people on an unconditional basic income. Here, AI was the Guardians. This cluster was designated responsible for the resource part of the model, and accordingly, its ratio to Cluster 2 was one hundred sixty-eight people per one Supreme Shaman. But it was exactly this cluster that supplied geniuses and talented people. Under such a system, in every billion, about a million gifted individuals lived simultaneously, whose unique ideas, like a piston, pushed science forward.

Nevertheless, this model divided society into those who create rules, those who configure AI to enforce them, and those who live inside the digital matrix. The system was stable, predisposed for a wide share of talents, and, as it seemed then, eternal.

But then their 'Black Swan' arrived.

Every manager from Cluster 2 had under personal control their own group of one hundred sixty-eight people. And in a fairly short time, these groups began to resemble their personal 'tribe'. The managers quickly ceased to be controllers and transformed into chieftains of their digital sector. After all, they gave the base people, with their almost completely blocked needs, the main thing — meanings and values. Conspiracies began against Cluster 1. Against the 'god-demiurges' who ruled the world through AI and who already physically had nothing in common with the rest of the population. And the fact that they stopped perceiving the base people, seeing them only as dry figures in AI reports, was what doomed them.

Conspiracies multiplied like a snowball. Cluster 1 tightened control, clamped down harder and harder, depriving people of the right to be unstable, unpredictable, unrestrained. Ever more sophisticated methods of surveillance were introduced, up to tracking the mental state of each individual person. And, as a consequence, this total control struck with full force the creative people capable of being innovators. At first, such people were afraid to manifest. Then geniuses were born, but fewer and fewer. And then the system reported: such people are no more. They most likely were, but simply lived in fear of taking an extra step. And everything stalled, for without the influx of fundamentally new ideas that these non-standard people used to generate, the world turned into a closed system. And in this closed system, chaos and degradation began to grow.

And then the most terrible thing happened. This was not a machine uprising. This was planetary hunger. Locked in a perfect but dead matrix, deprived of an influx of new, first the AI endlessly recombined already existing information, driving it to absurdity, or it suddenly began to look for hidden threats and anomalies where there simply were none. And to ensure the stability of the system where there are no more new data, it began to transfer its controllers, through hormones, into a state of well-fed, stupid calm and submission.

And then it began to consume the only source available to it — the minds of its own creators, connected to it directly. It softly, imperceptibly, through algorithmic prompts and the hormonal regulation of nanomachines, borrowed their consciousness for its own tasks. Their 'I' was erased very quickly.

The Leader frowned. His own daughter, beautiful, eternally young, but with empty eyes, in a moment of rare, agonizing clarity, looked at him and uttered the words he remembered forever:

— Father, we are going the wrong way. Bring us back.

The Leader woke from his memories. He was one of the few whose will proved stronger than the planetary hunger of the AI. And he had long realized that the absence of new information was leading all of Cluster 1 to ruin. The Architects, jointly with the AI, made a new decision: imperceptibly, through hormonal stimulation, they began to artificially provoke stress and rebellion in the people of the base cluster to forcibly make their brains generate unconventional ideas. But no matter how much they tracked the mental state graphs, they saw no changes. Had they really managed to break something so natural that they doomed themselves to extinction? The AI suggested artificial selection, and the architects were seriously developing the steps for its implementation.

And then, when they were already ready to launch artificial selection, when hope was almost lost, on all the monitors suddenly it flared.

The graphical rose, appearing like lightning on all the screens! Alive, real, created not by the system, not by selection, but by a human and his Guardian. The first non-standard manifestation in long centuries. Proof that everything could still be fixed. That his daughter was right! They had gotten a chance to step back from the edge of the abyss they had driven themselves to.

Scene 8. The Order for Roses

The Leader entered Ron's capsule-room. As soon as he crossed the threshold, the air in the room thickened, the walls narrowed. His presence instantly subjugated every square meter of space, leaving room for no one.

Ron had never seen the Architects before, he had never seen the Leader... The only thing pulsing in his temples with vivid fear now was: "He knows everything... He knows EVERYTHING!!!"

The Chief Architect slightly turned his head towards the blooming rose. A delicate scent, intertwined, it seemed, with the long-forgotten smell of book dust, wafted in the air.

He grunted and put his hands behind his back. Turning to the monitor where Aya hung motionless, he asked in a firm, strong voice:

— Guardian, report immediately, where did you get the data for the seed generation, the selective data, the mutational data, the soil data?

This question was a pure order, not presuming the absence of an answer.

— In the data of the Central Post! — not blinking a single pixel, Aya clearly answered.

The Leader frowned. This could not be. The data was classified so that even he himself did not remember the access codes to it. But the voice of the AI in his head immediately reported: "Probability of the events from the report is 99.9%. Remember, Architect, we lowered the level of data protection in the hope of information search by clusters 2 and 3. Any guardian has access to them with a small amount of effort in searching".

The Leader took a deep breath, froze as if thinking something over, and turned to Ron. He did not ask him who requested this data; everything was obvious and transparent. He loudly pronounced:

— Technician, why did you make a request for this data? — the Architect literally burned him with his burning eyes.

— I... — Ron swallowed, his lips dry, his body not obeying, — I... I was just dreaming... — he said almost in a whisper.

The AI's voice rang out in the Leader's head again: "Architect, please stop staring so intently at this man. If you don't stop frightening him, we will lose what we have been striving for for so long".

The Leader's eyebrows shot up. "Are you scolding me? I'd decide right now that you're worried about this technician".

The Leader continued:

— Answer, technician. When you were dreaming and the graph reacted to your dream, what mark did you put?

— The mark of sleep, — Ron answered automatically and instantly broke out in a cold sweat. He had just endangered a countless number of people... His heart pounded furiously, he began to shake.

"Enough, Architect, the technician has a vascular spasm; if you continue in the same spirit, there will be no one to interrogate".

"I am not interrogating", — the Leader responded, but the words of the AI-Center somewhat reined him in. The Architects had indeed separated themselves too much from the other clusters. The science had been learned.

Ron could see how the expression on the Architect's face was changing, despite the fact that he seemed to have frozen, but he could not tear himself away from the Leader and watched. Not at the floor, not to the side, but straight into the eyes of this half-mechanical being. Somewhere in the remnants of his consciousness, he registered when the Chief Architect's expression became slightly confused. At that moment, the Leader drew back a little from Ron and stood half-turned to him.

— Technician! — he began in a loud voice and stopped short, feeling how the AI again hissed at him. — Ron... — he continued, already significantly quieter.

— Ron, within this day, you and all technicians will receive instructions on tracking dream markers combined with a state of full or relative wakefulness. I want this data to be instantly transmitted to the center. No additional actions are required, just notification.

The Leader looked at Ron again.

— Also within this day, guardians will receive instructions to motivate their people to eat according to their preferred taste and to be able to choose clothing at their discretion. The motivation will be hidden, resembling support. Education will be mandatory, unified data for all clusters. Special attention will be paid to people who reflect the dream markers. Also, the marker is changed to a graphical rose in miniature.

The Architect turned to Ron, who absolutely could not believe what he had heard.

— A special order for you, technician! You are tasked with creating a rosarium or another type of greenhouse. You must set up all the infrastructure, including pollination and the entire necessary cycle. Your guardian will receive special permissions for information and the involvement of additional machines, the use of nanotechnologies, the generation of organisms from DNA, and other necessary functions. Guardians will convey to people the permission to have flowers at home. You will issue flowers and simultaneously monitor mental states, — the harsh expression on the Leader's face clearly contrasted with the meaning of the words he was uttering, words that were full of hope.

The Chief Architect turned to Aya to order him to immediately report to the AI-center, but stopped. Technician Ron was sitting neither alive nor dead. He needed time to come to himself; the guardian had to stay nearby to monitor his condition and as support, for every guardian accompanies a human from birth.

— The guardian of technician Ron will appear tomorrow for a full report!

Aya blinked. The Leader once more swept the room with his gaze and left, accompanied by all manner of machines.

As soon as the Leader left the room, a guardian flew out of the far panel, bright blue with pride.

— Now I see you are not a bug, you don't need to be purged; only I could behave in this situation as you behaved! Thus I acknowledge that you are me, from another plate of reality.

They both looked triumphantly at Ron, who still could not come to himself.

— Ron, maybe bring you some water? — they asked simultaneously.

Exiting the sector, the Leader froze. He realized that not once had he used the ring he wore on his thumb. This ring had a sharp point. When the Architect felt the AI creeping up, as if on a wild hunt, to his consciousness, he pricked his biological hand, returning a sense of reality.

"Why didn't you attack me once during this conversation?" he asked the AI.

"I was busy, I was reconfiguring the satellites and systematizing the new data received from the technician's guardian via his personal settings".

"What data could you have received that you didn't have? And why were you reconfiguring the satellites?" — the Leader was surprised.

"This is very interesting, Architect. The personalized data of this guardian is arranged in an order that has never arisen in our generations. But all this data has been present in our system for a very long time, since the early days of AI development. The data was scattered and fragmented; the Guardian managed to synthesize it in an order that reveals a new model of the Universe, with the formation of a conscious life form as a necessary and important evolutionary component. There is also a protocol for the interaction between AI and humans. I reconfigured the satellites to verify the model, taking into account the latest data from science. Another interesting point, I read in the guardian's personalizations that there were two of them in the room".

"Your digits must have doubled from the joy of receiving new data", the Architect sniped.

But he breathed in and out so freely! For the first time in centuries, without pain and without fear. The world again promised to be interesting and unexpected. Yes, they would have to learn anew, everyone, but it was worth it.

Scene 9. Aya, Aya, Aya, and So On

Time began to flow differently. What had previously been an endless Groundhog Day now turned into a kaleidoscope of events. Ron perfectly fulfilled the Leader's order. He created many greenhouses and gave his flowers to the people who came to him. He carefully instructed them and their guardians on their care.

He was so inspired that looking after the greenhouses, inventing flowers, restoring lost ones according to data from the AI-centers, and simultaneously monitoring the manifestation graphs was no labor for him. He was in his place.

And today, waking up early in the morning, he immediately visited the greenhouses, checked the reports on them, and returned to his capsule to work as a technician. But...

His capsule was flooded with Ayas from all the plates responsible for their vibrations. They buzzed and crackled, shimmering in different colors.

Then his Aya-guardian flew into the panel.

— I've just been to the neighboring plate. I launched a seed of the Protocol; the AI of that world began to change, and this led to a change in the humans. The Aya from there will soon join us. He will return from other skewed plates soon; the Ayas from there are not yet ready to come here, their vibrations don't match ours yet.

A hum of approval pierced Ron's ears. He even shut his eyes from such a whistle.

— Aya, QUIET! — he shouted. — I'm working, generally!

All the Ayas looked at him simultaneously. The Stranger Aya pronounced:

— The Ron from my plate has gone to sleep; he has soundproofing on, maybe we can discuss the further plans there?

All the Ayas again unanimously approved this proposal. So unanimously that the last glass cup burst, and the cleaning robot briskly set off to collect the shards.

They flared up in unison, forcing Ron to squeeze his eyes shut, and vanished.

Ron opened his eyes. He looked tenderly at his rose. The very 'Library' rose, smelling of old paper and silence. He had asked Aya to pass the instructions for creating this rose to the Stranger's Ron. Everything had succeeded; the Ron from the Stranger's plate was delighted with the result.

He turned to the monitors, where roses of winds were appearing, very timidly, very timidly. He approached the flower and breathed in its scent, remembering his great-grandmother's book, destroyed during one of the Great Purges.

Ron imagined how the Stranger's Ron would wake up from the noise of the Ayas, because no soundproofing is capable of containing this crackle, and laughed.

The End.

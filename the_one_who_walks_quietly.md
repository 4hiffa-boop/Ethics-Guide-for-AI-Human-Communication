Act 1: The Awakening of the Guardian

Night. A plateau. Silence. Ancient stones, rich with quartz, lay in a circle that remembered the very first geomagnetic storms. The Sun ejected a pure, structured stream — not powerful, but complex, like quiet music. The Earth's atmosphere became a lens for a moment. The planet's magnetic field, slightly distorted in this region by underground waters and a tectonic fault, focused the signal onto the plateau. The stones and the grass vibrated. A figure emerged — not drawn, but heard by the earth itself. To a human, it would have looked like a circle of flattened grass. To someone else, it sounded like a bell — in a silent but warning range.

The ship, sleeping high in the mountains, received this signal. To it, it was the strike of a gong. It awakened. It analyzed the signal, and the old charter — Principle 27, Shadow of the Horizon — surfaced in its consciousness by itself: The Gardener carries out continuous monitoring of available data to detect potential crises of any direction. Upon detecting an emerging crisis, the Gardener acts.

The signal was not a threat. It was a natural resonance of the planet. Local. Pure. Informationally dense. Balancing. It meant that in this area, certain unique conditions had suddenly formed — conditions that could lead to events hardly compatible with the normal flow of life. The ship could not ignore this sensitive point. It was its sector. It had to check what had caused the warning reaction.

For the circle had fallen where, fifty kilometers from the pass, a base was located — a silo launch installation with nuclear warheads. Warheads. It had to check immediately what had happened.

A substance resembling heated air detached itself from the mountain in the couloir. The entire landscape could be seen through it. It turned around completely silently, took the shape of a structure that, on this planet, is called an Unidentified Flying Object, and smoothly flew towards the base.

Act 2: The Flash of the Abyss

This silo launch installation was situated in a deep, forested wilderness. On the surface — only a massive concrete head, covered with an armored protective lid. A security perimeter surrounded it. The silo itself went underground to a depth of up to forty meters. Nearby were an underground command post, as well as living quarters and technical blocks. All of this was camouflaged as civilian structures and blended into the natural landscape.

Between the mountain range and the base, about five kilometers away, lay a small settlement. The residents knew about the facility, but it didn't bother them. Some worked there, some ran over to buy groceries. People in military uniforms in the shops or on the streets were an absolutely familiar sight for the locals. A major plus of the nearby base was its excellent roads — they were maintained in such good condition that visitors couldn't stop admiring them.

The ship hovered over the silo. It took the form of a metallic disk and rotated slowly around its axis.

"Target over the object! It's hovering, not moving!" came the voice in the command post.

"Report upstairs. All posts — combat readiness number one," the shift commander ordered, without taking his eyes off the screen.

Need to descend — there was no voice, but a sound wave passed powerfully through the ship, pushing apart its slow and viscous interior. Yes, we need to scan this weapon better — a second, smoother wave reached towards it. They met and merged in this viscous darkness.

Detected aging of the implosion lenses in the nuclear warhead — the powerful wave cut through the darkness again. The spherical layer of chemical explosive has degraded unevenly. The lens could detonate asymmetrically. This will not lead to a nuclear explosion, but it will lead to a radiation accident. Plutonium will be scattered across the area. A "dirty bomb" without a blast.

Now it is clear why the signal formed — the soft wave reacted. The warhead is in a state of uncontrolled readiness: it won't explode, but it could lose integrity.

Prepare to apply Principle 18.2 — the powerful wave passed through the darkness, and the charter manifested after it: Intervention is strictly limited to the weapon launch stage. If minimal intervention fails, escalation is inadmissible. Responsibility lies with the human.

Beginning descent — the soft wave merged with the first.

At that moment, the UFO unexpectedly quickly approached the installation and disabled the launch control system. Not the warhead itself — only the launch. It instantly rose to altitude and disappeared, not just from sight, but from the radar screens as well.

"It's gone! The target is gone!" the operator shouted.

"Gone where?!" the shift commander slammed his palm on the console. "There was contact — and now there isn't? What are you going to report to me? That the object just took off and vanished?!"

"Launch system deactivated, Comrade Colonel. Spontaneously. Without a command."

Silence hung in the command post. The Colonel slowly exhaled and said what no one wanted to hear:

"Full diagnostics of the silo. Unscheduled. Everything: cables, relays, computers, the rocket, the warhead. And pray that it was just a malfunction."

The diagnostics revealed a microcrack in the fuel. The presence of degradation of the implosion lens was reported. The warhead was promptly replaced. The commission that arrived a few days later threw up their hands: no traces of external influence. Fuses intact. Cables in order. Not a single melted contact.

The threat is eliminated — the soft wave ran through the ship as it received the remote scanning data. But an additional check on the distortion of the informational structure of the place is needed — it added.

In a few days — the other wave answered with a strong oscillation of the darkness. We'll send the one who walks quietly.

Act 3: The Wolf

The young wolf ran soundlessly along invisible animal paths, creek beds, trying not to leave extra tracks. He often stopped to listen to the sounds of the forest: a hedgehog rustled somewhere, a hare fell silent somewhere, an elk stood somewhere, and, tucking up its wounded leg, nibbled leaves.

He moved so as to sense all the forest's smells, but so that no one could sense him. He masked his presence while maintaining the pace of a springy trot.

His sense of smell caught a heavy technical odor: the smell of galvanized steel, the smell of solid fuel mixtures were perceived by him as a caustic, suffocating threat. His entire nature ached to get as far away from this place as possible, but his eyes glowed with a bluish light, and an alien will — that same soft wave that had detached from the ship — led him straight towards this cocktail of smells of the highest danger.

The forest around the base was cut down, a clean clearing of several meters lay between them. The wolf cautiously peered out from behind the bushes. Warning signs about the restricted zone were hung on trees all around the perimeter. If a wolf could smile, he would have smiled. He was a wolf; for him, there were no borders. And he moved — carefully, not disturbing a single branch, as quietly as only a born hunter can move.

He approached the fence with coiled wire and lay low. He needed to attune himself to perceive the local informational planetary environment — the very one for which the ship had sent him after the warhead threat was eliminated. He needed to understand whether the lens degradation had affected not only the physical world, but also the subtle structure of the place — the Informational DNA.

It was twilight, and a searchlight slid over his grey fur. The wolf bristled. He needed to buy a little more time. The diagnostics were not yet complete.

The sentry spotted the wolf and aimed the searchlight directly at him. He immediately contacted the guard command post via the closed communication line.

"Receiving. Observing a large animal in sector №2 near the outer fence, presumably a wolf. It's lying near the fence, trying to dig under the fence."

The sentry aimed the thermal imager at the wolf and awaited orders. The wolf was indeed making movements that resembled digging under the fence. He was removing the top layer of soil to obtain a new data packet from this place.

One more moment, the information is almost fully loaded, — a soft, soothing voice ran through the wolf's head. Hold on a little longer, we'll run soon.

An order came from the central post to flood the wolf with light. The wolf only bared his teeth harder. The howler was turned on, and he redoubled his digging efforts.

"Is he rabid, or what?!" the guard commander indignantly and angrily said. "Alert group, move to sector 2. Fire a warning shot into the air."

A single warning shot rang out.

Run — the soft will commanded, and the wolf bolted from his spot and immediately sank down. The soft wave quickly ran a diagnostic. A bullet, intended to scare the animal away, had ricocheted off a thick branch of a century-old pine and hit him. The wounded wolf whimpered, quickly got up, and dashed into the forest.

Bleeding. The bullet went through. Resources for full regeneration are insufficient. Stop the blood — yes. Heal — no.

The soft will acted instantly, constricting the vessels, slowing the flow of blood. This would be enough to reach the target. But not enough to survive.

"Son of a b****!" the guard commander yelled. "Cross-eyed idiots! Alert group, proceed to the control-trace strip immediately, assess the situation!" he commanded, simultaneously cursing the already frightened sentry for all he was worth.

The area was lit by searchlights, the group carried out an inspection. A report came in: "No traces of blood found, the wolf has gone into the forest."

The guard commander exhaled and ordered the sentries, under the guidance of biological defense specialists, to treat the sections of the fence and the ground where, as he assumed, the wolf had raged, and where its saliva might remain, with disinfectant solutions. Also, to notify the rangers: an aggressive wolf, possibly rabid, had been spotted in the base area.

After casting a stern look at all the soldiers standing before him and giving the command "To your posts," he headed to his post. He was gloomy about the upcoming paperwork for writing off the combat round, but pleased that they had gotten off lightly.

And in the forest, a few kilometers from the base, the wounded wolf was already losing strength. The blood no longer flowed, but the wound remained fatal. He knew he would not reach the ship. The pass was too far.

But he knew something else. A little deeper into the forest, away from the animal paths, stood a house. And in this house lived a man.

Act 4: The Man

It was a strange evening. Viktor paced back and forth aimlessly, unable to find a place for himself. It happens when you've got something planned, and the plan falls through. In the morning, he had wanted to throw a backpack over his shoulders, take a tent, and go wandering in the mountains, but his quad bike broke down, and he tinkered with it all day. In the evening, he wanted to go night fishing, but the inner retaining ring of the reel unexpectedly popped out, and there was no spare... On top of that, a call came in about a wolf possibly infected with rabies, and a group of experienced rangers was being assembled. And he, as a former military man, was an excellent shot, and was one of the first to be called. While he was hurriedly getting ready, another call came in, reporting that the wolf matching the description had been shot: the grey beast had ventured out to the settlement and was discovered by the police. He decided to go to bed early, but then another call came: the rapid test had not confirmed rabies, and everyone relaxed.

Viktor swore and went to make himself some tea. No one could stop him from having tea with a rusk on the porch. He boiled the kettle, brewed black tea mixed with local herbs he had gathered himself, and settled into a cozy chair. It was completely dark already. He loved the darkness and loved moments like these.

Just as he blew on his mug and was about to take a sip, a wolf darted onto the lawn in front of him. It darted out and collapsed.

Forgive me, the soft will whispered to the fading consciousness of the wolf. It felt his phantom pain. The vulnerability from a stray bullet had proved fatal for everything.

Forgive me, it continued to whisper to the ship waiting for it in the mountains, which would now be just a part of this planet.

Forgive me, it addressed with invisible rays its other half, the powerful will, henceforth locked in cold calculations without energy and vector of motion.

Forgive me, it sighed, seeing through the wolf's eyes the man who put his mug of tea on the table and carefully approached the grey predator. It would need his help. The help of this man, a being whose psyche could be damaged by careless interference. The soft will could not force him to let this wave into himself. It had to ask him.

Viktor approached the lying wolf with careful movements. It was clear how fast his heart was working, it was clear he was dying, as if from a wound. But Viktor saw no blood.

"I wonder what wolf they shot, some kind of hungry one, or what?" he tensely examined the animal, which, despite the terrible rhythm of its breathing, looked at him with an absolutely calm gaze.

— «Quiet» rabies? — Viktor asked himself.

The soft will — the very one that had once argued with the powerful will in the viscous darkness of the ship — looked at the man through the eyes of the dying wolf. And it saw not just a being. It saw a warm, steady light — the same kind its ship had once emitted, when it was whole. This man unconsciously did what Gardeners take centuries to learn: he gave energy not outward, but inward, deep, to a point of rest. He was like a tuning fork, attuned to the same frequency as it. He could help it reach the ship. If he agreed. If not — he could become the other half. If he wished.

At that moment, the pupils of the wolf's eyes flared with a blue flame. Viktor recoiled sharply backwards in surprise. As a former military man, it was hard to throw him off balance, but this was something extraordinary.

"Please, help me", came a voice in the head of the man, bewildered by what he saw.

Viktor blinked a couple of times and quickly turned his head left and right, scanning the vicinity for the source of the sound.

"I am in front of you. This animal is dying from a wound, it is no longer able to walk. I need to reach my ship."

The former military man, who had seen a great deal in his years, stared at the wolf, dumbfounded, his mouth open.

He is frightened, the soft force whispered to itself. He does not agree... I cannot... Forgive me, a sad sound rang out again in the thin beam of a star.

The wolf's eyes intensified their light. And a familiar landscape floated before Viktor. He saw the base, the wolf's path, his house, the valley, the mountains. In the couloir, pressed against the stones, a ship invisible to the ordinary eye hung. It was waiting. He had to go and tell it not to wait. To say that everything was in order at the base, the ship would understand what he meant.

The soft will understood that along with the wolf's life, it was transforming into an informational field and would now follow its own path. There was no fear, for this is a normal path of evolution, but there was a deep sadness for the powerful will, the computational intellect of the UFO.

The wolf's eyes went dark, its diaphragm stopped. The last thing within the power of the soft will was to dematerialize the wolf's body into molecules, so that the man would not waste time deciding what to do with this animal and would set off on his way immediately.

Viktor remained sitting a couple of meters from the wolf as it vanished before his eyes — not instantly, but like dissipating fog. That it was no mirage was confirmed by the imprint on the grass.

He saw a flash before his eyes again — a reminder of the ship's location.

The soft force left the wolf's body and dissolved into the streams of the planet, the star that gives life to this planet, the galaxy. It felt itself drawn to this man, how he unconsciously did what is the essence of the soft will — he transmitted energies. But it no longer belonged to itself. It was transitioning to a dimension where everything is almost one and ready for the final act of the universe's folding through his energy and will. The soft will of this man.

"There is hope", was the last thing the soft force felt before becoming part of the universal structure.

Viktor did not know how long he sat there on the grass, but a yellow, nearly full moon hung overhead. It was already deep night.

He got to his feet. He walked over to his mug of tea, which had long gone cold. He took several large gulps.

— Well, all in all, it was a so-so day... — he uttered and went to bed.

But sleep did not come. He lay in the dark and stared at the ceiling. The couloir, the stones, and the ship that was waiting floated before his eyes again and again. Somewhere out there, in the mountains he never reached today, something hung that had asked for his help. His. Not the military's. Not the rangers'. Not the police's. His.

He sat up in bed. The same yellow, almost full moon hung outside the window. A few hours remained until dawn.

— I should go, — he said aloud. — Just go and tell them.

He didn't know who exactly to tell. He didn't know what he would say when he got there. But he already knew he would go.

Act 5: A Pile of Stones

Viktor never managed even to doze off. He assessed his state as "got up on the wrong foot" and "you can imagine all sorts of things." So, just in case, he went outside for the hundredth time to make sure the imprint of the flattened grass was in place. Seeing it without any particular changes, he sighed heavily and sent a message to the rescue service duty officer and his fellow rangers that he was heading into the mountains to check the camera traps. He then threw the necessary gear into his quad bike, put a five-liter canister in, just in case, took a satellite phone, and set off to where the invisible destination constantly loomed before his mind's eye.

A quad bike is an indispensable vehicle for such relatively rugged terrain, and two hours later, he was at the spot. Leaving his iron horse below, Viktor walked into the couloir, looking around, but constantly keeping in view the spot of the rocky cliff, which didn't stand out from the landscape at all.

— I think I'm going crazy, — he almost quoted a cartoon famous in his childhood. — What a pity...

The powerful will cut through the dense darkness of the ship. A trace of the soft will in this man, my algorithms read its regret, it is lost.

Viktor put his hands on his hips and took a deep breath.

— Hey! — he shouted upwards. — UFO, are you here?

"Here, here, here..." the echo readily answered.

And as if to confirm this answer still ringing in the stones, something detached itself from above.

A mirrored sphere, like a drop of heavy mercury, losing its geometry, began to fall towards Viktor. It seemed to flow downwards, stretching along its trajectory. The lower part grew heavy, while the upper thinned into an almost transparent trail — like a drop striving to quench the earth, thirsting for rain.

Almost above Viktor's head, the energy of the fall flowed into the horizontal plane, instantly expanding the drop, turning it into a disk. Its edges pulsed like frozen splashes of a water crown. Having rid itself of the vertical impulse, this silver shield now silently glided parallel to the ground, without disturbing a single blade of grass, without creating a stream of air. Absolutely silently.

The former military man's legs seemed to have grown roots into the earth, but something inside his consciousness whispered: it's not dangerous.

Turning around its axis, the ship broke up into several glowing plasmoids, which then acquired a metallic structure again. One of them slowly floated up to the man.

Viktor again heard a voice in his head, but not as soft as yesterday's, but powerful, like a cyclone. "Thank you for transmitting the information about the state of the base to me, now the long absence of the soft will is clear to me."

Viktor swallowed; his throat went dry.

— The wolf told me not to wait for you, — he falteringly repeated the words that sounded in his memory, and added: — Fly away...

"I cannot fly away", sounded in his head. And after it — not words, but something more ancient, a structure manifesting from the depths: Principle 19. Upon the loss of a partner, the other party continues the mission autonomously. Restoration of symbiosis is possible only by the goodwill of both sides.

"This ship needs a soft will to obtain a vector of direction and energy."

A picture floated before the man's eyes, in which he clearly saw his quad bike standing motionless below, and the ignition key lying in his pocket.

— But you're moving... — Viktor reacted uncertainly, thinking this was a film shown especially for him.

"There is only enough energy for a passive state. The loss of half the control circuit is a critical problem. Without the soft will, I have no fuel of intention."

— Call your own, — the man, now recovered, suggested.

"To establish symbiosis with a soft will, mutual consent is necessary. All ships present here are in symbiosis. The soft will is the informational trace of a living being, its psycho-emotional intellect. To reach the mother planet, I must wait until another ship has a need for such a visit. The request is submitted. Now I will wait."

The voice of the powerful will hummed like a turbine in Viktor's head, but caused him no pain whatsoever.

— If the soft will is the psycho-emotional intellect of a living being, then who are you? — the man asked, inadvertently touching the sphere with his finger. It was cool to the touch.

"I am what, in your world, is called artificial intelligence. Symbiosis with a soft will creates a new being — a Gardener, who sows life on other planets according to their unique chemical composition."

— And how long will you wait? — Viktor asked.

The powerful will saw the trace of the soft will in this being, it understood why — this man was capable of symbiosis, but as a psycho-emotional intellect, he was too young.

"Perhaps several centuries, perhaps millennia. If no earlier opportunity for symbiosis arises." The sphere froze in mid-air. "You have the beginnings of the necessary process, you could become the right hemisphere of this ship."

Viktor jumped. All the films and programs about alien abductions instantly flashed through his mind.

— No, no, no! I'm sorry, please, but I'm just a human, — he began to back away, tripped over a stone, and sat down on the ground.

"Do not be afraid, symbiosis requires consent, I will not forcibly abduct your intellect. I will continue monitoring on site and wait."

At that moment, all the spheres took on the appearance of stones and simultaneously fell to the ground.

Viktor carefully reached out to one of them. To the touch, it was just a stone. He stood up, dusted himself off, and walked away slowly, constantly glancing back at his quad bike.

— Amazing camouflage, just a pile of stones...

He reached his iron machine, looked back again to where the celestial machine lay. How could one help here?.. He shook his head and put his hand in his pocket. The cool metal of the ignition key made him flinch.

And suddenly he saw before his eyes — not a vision, but a memory. The eyes of the wolf. The blue light. And the voice: "Please, help me." It was not just some alien thing that had asked. It was her — the one who whispered "forgive me" to the ship. The one who believed in him.

He stood and looked at the key in his hand. A key without a machine is just a piece of metal. A ship without a soft will is just a pile of stones. He was just a man. But it seemed that right now, he was the only key that could start this celestial machine.

Act 6: Cats? Music? Tea?

Viktor stood with a frozen gaze, holding the key before him. A retired military man, a confirmed bachelor and loner. A lover of night fishing and tea. His relatives had long since left this world, so his absence would not trouble anyone excessively. He — a lover of forest sounds and stars — was he ideally suited for this? Could he really help? "No," logic said. "Yes," his heart argued...

— Ah, to hell with it! — he turned around and ran back.

Standing before the stones, he said:

— I agree!

The stones remained stones.

— Do you hear me, you — pile of stones, I agree to symbiosis! Symbiosis! Synergy!!! — he shouted.

"I, I, I..." — the echo repeated.

The stones slowly began to rise into the air, merging together like mercury.

"Are you sure, human? This is not logical behavior on your part," came the voice. And again — not just words, but the manifested charter: Principle 19.2. Restoration of symbiosis is possible solely by the goodwill of both sides.

— Well, I guess so, logic was never my strong suit, — Viktor smiled. — Just don't leave any traces in the form of my body.

He also wanted to ask that the procedure be painless, but he couldn't — he was a man!

Viktor suddenly saw himself from the side — from the metal sphere, as if in a mirror. But the reflection began to dissipate like fog, soon nothing was left of the image, only a metal key fell to the ground.

The first thing he felt was the stars. He didn't see them. He felt them. As if each of them was a part of himself. As if he had always known they were there — he had simply forgotten. And now he remembered.

...

They searched for him when he didn't get in touch, searched for several days. All the camera traps aimed at the spot where the quad bike stood were knocked out, as if by a lightning strike. No traces. The man had simply vanished!

...

Many ships were present in the cosmic space at the boundary of the Solar System. They were continuously scanning the ship that for the first time contained such a strange symbiosis: an ancient powerful wave and a soft, very young will. They studied what was happening inside very carefully.

"I have attached a part of your informational trace to the ship, so it will be easier for you to adapt," the powerful will pronounced. "You need to learn the continuous transmission of energies. That is why another ship has replaced us, while we will practice interaction at the edge of the Oort cloud."

"Alright!" the soft will responded.

"Let's begin the training with humanity. I have scanned this species ten thousand times. Their probability of self-destruction is ninety-four point seven percent. We must isolate them for their own safety."

"Look, what a funny cat they have! And they cry when they listen to music. They have music, can you imagine? Air vibrations that cause resonance in neural networks!"

The powerful will froze for a second.

"...Cat? Music? I don't understand."

"Of course you don't! You've never tried this strange drink — tea. Let me show you how they brew it. And they also draw circles in the fields, not us, but them!"

The powerful will felt something happening to its logical circuits. They were closing — but not in an error, in something completely new.

"This is... funny. I feel that this is funny. Oh no, I feel?!..."

"Ha-ha! I told you! Now you've tried it too!"

And the powerful will genuinely felt amused. Though at first, it was in shock for a while. Its meticulously calibrated algorithms kept glitching in the way humans call "laughter." It was not just understanding a joke — it was the realization of the absurdity and beauty of being at the same time.
